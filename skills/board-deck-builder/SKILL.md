# Board Deck Builder

A board deck serves a different purpose than a pitch deck — the board already knows and is invested in the company; the job here is efficient, honest information transfer and clear decision requests, not persuasion from a standing start.

## Standard structure (adapt, don't force every section every time)

1. **TL;DR / Summary** — the 3-5 things the board needs to walk away knowing, up front. Board members are busy; don't make them extract the summary themselves from 20 slides.
2. **Key metrics** — the handful of numbers that actually indicate business health for this specific company/stage, shown with trend (not just a snapshot), and compared against the plan/target the board previously agreed to.
3. **Progress against prior quarter's stated goals** — explicitly close the loop on what was committed last time, including honest reporting on what wasn't hit and why.
4. **Challenges/risks** — surfaced proactively, not buried or omitted. A board that's surprised by a problem it wasn't warned about loses trust faster than a board told about a problem directly, even a serious one.
5. **The ask** — if there's a specific decision or resource the board needs to provide (budget approval, a hire sign-off, guidance on a strategic question, a fundraise decision), state it explicitly and early enough that the discussion has time to actually happen, not squeezed in at the end.
6. **Appendix** — supporting detail for anything the board might want to dig into, kept out of the main flow so the core narrative stays tight.

## Principles

- **Lead with the number that matters most for this company's stage** (e.g. burn/runway for an early company, growth rate for a scaling one, margin for a maturing one) — don't present a uniform metrics dashboard regardless of what's actually decision-relevant right now.
- **Bad news belongs on slide 2, not slide 15.** Boards can help most when they see problems early; framing that buries or softens a miss until deep in the deck reads as evasive even if unintentional.
- **Every metric needs a comparison point** — a number without a trend line or target comparison doesn't tell the board whether it's good or bad.
- **Keep the deck itself lean; put depth in the appendix or verbal discussion** — a board deck that tries to be comprehensive on every slide loses the signal in noise.

## What NOT to do

- Don't spin a miss as a win through selective framing — boards see through this quickly, and it costs more trust long-term than direct reporting of a genuine shortfall.
- Don't bury the ask — if there's a decision needed, it should be unmistakable, not something the board has to infer from context.

## Output format

```markdown
## Board deck outline: <quarter/meeting>

**Summary (top of deck):** [3-5 key takeaways]

**Section-by-section outline:**
1. Summary
2. Key metrics — [which metrics, with trend/target comparison]
3. Progress vs. prior goals
4. Challenges/risks
5. The ask — [specific decision needed, if any]
6. Appendix — [what goes here]
```
